# Go-To-Market Strategy — Pasadena AI Institute

> **Pasadena AI Institute, hosted by Whistle Labs.**
> Internal operating document. Goal: fill the Founding Cohort (24 seats, Fall 2026) at $995/seat through local, relationship-driven outreach — not paid ads.
> Last updated: June 2026.

---

## 1. Executive summary

- **The play is local and human, not paid and digital.** We sell a two-day, in-person, hands-on AI conference in Pasadena to professionals 35–60. The product is built on trust and proximity, so the go-to-market is too: rooms, handshakes, referrals, and free intro sessions — in that order. Paid ads are a backup lever, parked in the appendix.
- **The free "lunch & learn" is our single highest-leverage tool.** A 60–90 minute free intro session is both the marketing and the proof. It demonstrates "until it clicks" in real time and converts warm rooms into paid seats. Everything else exists to fill those rooms.
- **We only need 24 people.** This changes the whole strategy. We do not need scale; we need ~24 right-fit humans out of a region of millions. Roughly 8–12 well-run touchpoints (chamber talks, Rotary visits, library workshops, BNI referrals) can plausibly produce a full cohort. We optimize for depth of relationship, not reach.
- **Whistle Labs is the credibility engine.** "Hosted by Whistle Labs, a company that actually builds AI software" answers the unspoken question — *who are you to teach this?* — before it's asked. We lead with the host on first mention everywhere.
- **The funnel has two doors and one promise.** Ready-to-buy people hit Stripe directly ("Reserve your seat — $995"). Everyone else enters via the interest form → short Zoom call → personal payment link. The Zoom call is where most Founding Cohort seats will actually close, because this audience buys from people, not pages.

## 2. Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) & segments

**Core ICP:** A professional, business owner, or executive aged ~35–60 in Pasadena / Greater LA / Southern California who knows AI matters, feels behind or skeptical, has disposable income, and wants a trusted, in-person, no-judgment way to finally *get it* — and ideally build something. Non-technical is the norm, not the exception.

Below are four concrete personas with where to find each.

### Persona A — "The Established Small-Business Owner"
- **Who:** Owns a local business with 5–50 employees — a dental/medical practice, law firm, real estate brokerage, accounting firm, marketing agency, construction/contracting company, restaurant group, boutique. Age 45–60. Cash-comfortable, time-poor, suspicious of hype, but watching competitors start to use AI.
- **What they want:** Practical wins — automating quotes, drafting contracts/marketing, customer follow-up, cutting admin hours. Leans **"I Have an Idea"** track (a tool or workflow for their business).
- **Where to find them:** **Pasadena Chamber of Commerce** events and member directory; **Rotary Club of Pasadena** and Altadena/San Marino/Arcadia Rotary; **BNI chapters** across the San Gabriel Valley; industry associations (local bar association, board of Realtors, dental society); business networking breakfasts; their CPA/attorney/banker (referral sources).

### Persona B — "The Mid-Career Corporate Professional"
- **Who:** Manager, director, or individual contributor at a larger employer — JPL/Caltech-adjacent contractors, Kaiser/Huntington Health, banks, insurance, Disney/entertainment back-office (Burbank/Glendale), Parsons, engineering and aerospace firms. Age 35–55. Worried about staying relevant; may want company to pay or may pay themselves.
- **What they want:** Career insurance and fluency. Wants to walk into Monday able to use AI in their actual job. Could go either track; many start **"Zero Experience."**
- **Where to find them:** **LinkedIn organic** (by far the best channel for this persona); professional associations (PMI Los Angeles, local marketing/HR/finance groups); coworking spaces; alumni networks; employer L&D departments (sell seats as professional development). Many will expense $995 as training.

### Persona C — "The Curious Pre-/Post-Retiree & Encore Careerist"
- **Who:** Age 55–65, financially secure, often a recent or near-retiree, consultant, board member, nonprofit leader, or someone starting a "second act" venture. Highly motivated, time-rich, *most* likely to say "I'm too old for this" — and most delighted when it clicks.
- **What they want:** To not be left out of a generational shift, to help grandkids/their nonprofit, or to launch a passion project. Strong **"Zero Experience"** fit; some have a clear idea.
- **Where to find them:** **Pasadena City College Continuing/Community Education**; **Pasadena Public Library** and branch libraries (program partnerships, flyers); **OLLI / lifelong-learning programs**; church and synagogue communities; service clubs (Rotary, Kiwanis, Lions); the **Pasadena Senior Center** (active, affluent demographic — not "seniors" in the frail sense).

### Persona D — "The Aspiring Founder / Side-Project Builder"
- **Who:** Age 35–50 with a specific business or product idea they've never been able to build because they "can't code." Frustrated by quotes from dev shops. The exact person vibe coding + Cursor + Claude was made for.
- **What they want:** To build a real prototype of their idea in two days. The flagship **"I Have an Idea"** persona — our best testimonials and referrers come from here.
- **Where to find them:** **Coworking spaces** (e.g., independent coworking and shared-office venues around Old Town and the Playhouse District); startup/founder meetups; **Whistle Labs' own network and inbound**; PCC and entrepreneurship programs; local accelerators/SCORE/SBDC; word of mouth from Persona A.

> **Prioritization for the Founding Cohort:** Personas A and D close fastest and refer best (clear ROI, existing local networks). Lead generation effort splits roughly: A 35%, B 25%, C 20%, D 20%.

## 3. Positioning & messaging

### The core promise
**Go from zero to one with AI — in two days, in person, in your own backyard. You'll leave having built something real.**

We are the opposite of endless YouTube videos and salesy webinars: a small room, real humans, and we stay until it clicks.

### The five messaging pillars
1. **Zero to one.** We start at absolute zero and end with something real in your hands. No experience required.
2. **Hands-on, in person.** A room, not a video. Small cohort, real attention, we don't move on until you get it.
3. **Two doors, one room.** Come to *learn* AI from scratch, or come to *build* your idea — we set up Claude, ChatGPT, and Cursor and teach you vibe coding, agents, and custom workflows.
4. **Local & community.** Proudly Pasadena, serving all of Southern California. You'll know the people in the room, and you join an alumni community when you leave.
5. **Honest & practical.** We tell you what's genuinely useful and what's hype. You're right on time — no fear-mongering.

### Credibility anchor (use everywhere)
Hosted by **Whistle Labs** (whistlelabs.ai), a company that builds AI software for a living. We don't just talk about AI — we ship it, and we'll sit next to you while you do too.

### Key objections + rebuttals

| Objection | Rebuttal (voice: warm, honest, plain) |
|---|---|
| **"I'm too old for this / I'm not technical."** | "Our most common attendee is a 50-something business owner who's never written a line of code. The whole point of *zero to one* is that we start at zero. We stay in the room until it clicks — that's the promise. You're right on time." |
| **"I'm too busy — two days is a lot."** | "Two focused days in person will save you months of half-watching videos that never stick. You block the time once, you walk out actually using AI. Most people tell us it was the most productive two days of their year." |
| **"$995 is a lot of money."** | "It's all-inclusive — two full days, lunches, all materials, your software set up and working, and an alumni community. One workflow you build can pay for it many times over. Compare it to a single afternoon with a consultant who builds nothing with you. Many attendees expense it as professional development." |
| **"I can learn this free on YouTube."** | "You can — and most people don't, because there's no room, no deadline, and no one to unstick you at 2pm when it breaks. We compress months of scattered videos into two days where you actually build the thing. The free version costs you the most: your time and the fact that it never quite happens." |
| **"Will this be outdated in six months?"** | "The tools change; the way of thinking doesn't. We teach you how to learn and adapt with AI, set up the current best tools (Claude, ChatGPT, Cursor), and you join a community that keeps you current after." |
| **"Who are you to teach this?"** | "We're hosted by Whistle Labs, a company that builds AI software professionally. This is what we do every day — now we sit down and do it with you." |

## 4. Channels — local, relationship-first (no ads)

Prioritized. The first four are the engine; the rest amplify. For a 24-seat cohort, going deep on a handful beats going wide on all.

> **The pattern that fits a 35–60 local audience:** they trust people and places they already know, attend in-person community institutions, respond to a warm introduction far more than a cold click, and are reachable through a small number of high-density nodes (a chamber, a Rotary, a library). Relationship-density, not reach, is the unlock.

### Tier 1 — The engine

#### 1. Free "Lunch & Learn" intro sessions (top-of-funnel anchor)
- **What to do:** Run free 60–90 minute intro sessions ("AI in 60 minutes — no experience required") hosted at partner venues (chamber, library, coworking space, a sponsor business's conference room). Teach one genuinely useful thing live, demo *zero to one*, then invite the room to the paid cohort.
- **First action:** Lock the first session date and a free venue (Pasadena Chamber or a library branch). Build a simple 45-min teach + 15-min "here's the cohort" deck.
- **Why it fits:** This audience needs to *experience* the room before paying $995. The free session is the trial. See the full playbook in Section 6.

#### 2. Pasadena Chamber of Commerce
- **What to do:** Join as a member (Whistle Labs / Pasadena AI Institute). Attend mixers and breakfasts, get on the events calendar, and offer to host a member lunch & learn. Use the member directory for warm outreach to Persona A.
- **First action:** Email/call the Chamber membership director this week; ask about (a) membership, (b) speaking/lunch-and-learn slots, (c) the next mixer. Show up to the next one in person.
- **Why it fits:** The Chamber *is* the room where Pasadena's established business owners (Persona A) already gather and trust each other. One good talk reaches dozens of right-fit people at once.

#### 3. Service clubs — Rotary, Kiwanis, Lions (speaking circuit)
- **What to do:** These clubs need a speaker every single week and love a relevant, non-salesy topic. Offer a 20–25 minute talk: "What AI actually means for your business — honestly." End with the free session invite, not a hard pitch.
- **First action:** Contact the program chair of the **Rotary Club of Pasadena** plus 2–3 nearby clubs (Altadena, San Marino, Arcadia, South Pasadena) to book talks over the next 6–8 weeks.
- **Why it fits:** Direct access to affluent, civically engaged 45–65 professionals and owners (Personas A and C), with implicit trust from the club setting. Each talk is a pre-warmed lead batch.

#### 4. BNI & referral networking groups
- **What to do:** Visit BNI and similar structured referral chapters across the San Gabriel Valley as a guest. These are built on referrals — exactly our growth motion. Aim to be the "AI person" members refer to.
- **First action:** Find 3–4 local BNI chapters, email the chapter presidents, and book guest visits. Bring a crisp one-line answer to "what do you do?"
- **Why it fits:** Members are local owners and professionals (Persona A) whose entire job in the group is sending each other business. One member can refer many.

### Tier 2 — Institutional partnerships

#### 5. Pasadena City College — Continuing/Community Education
- **What to do:** Explore a co-marketed or co-hosted intro session; at minimum, get listed/flyered in their continuing-ed channels. Position the Institute as the deeper, hands-on next step.
- **First action:** Contact the PCC Community Education / Extension office about partnership or guest-session options.
- **Why it fits:** PCC's continuing-ed audience is precisely Personas B and C — adults actively choosing to learn, who already trust the institution.

#### 6. Pasadena Public Library + branch libraries
- **What to do:** Offer to run a free public "Intro to AI" program; place flyers; get on the events calendar. Libraries actively seek free, useful adult programming.
- **First action:** Email the adult-programming coordinator at the Central Library to propose a free session.
- **Why it fits:** Trusted, free, neutral civic space; strong draw for Persona C and curious locals. Pure credibility, low cost.

#### 7. Industry & professional associations
- **What to do:** Offer to speak / run a member webinar or lunch & learn for groups whose members face AI disruption: local bar association, board of Realtors, dental/medical societies, CPA chapters, PMI-LA, AMA/marketing groups, HR associations.
- **First action:** Pick the 2 associations richest in our ICP (start with Realtors and a professional-services group) and pitch a member session.
- **Why it fits:** Pre-segmented, high-income professionals with concrete AI use cases; the association's endorsement transfers trust.

### Tier 3 — Amplifiers

#### 8. LinkedIn (organic only)
- **What to do:** The founder posts 3–4x/week: short, honest, useful AI insights; "here's a thing you can do today"; behind-the-scenes of building the Institute; cohort countdown. No ads — just genuine, plain-English value. DM warm connections personally.
- **First action:** Post a launch announcement of the Founding Cohort; pin it; connect with everyone met at in-person events.
- **Why it fits:** This is where Persona B lives and where professionals research credibility before saying yes. Organic posting compounds and costs only time.

#### 9. Coworking spaces
- **What to do:** Partner with independent coworking and shared-office venues around Old Town / Playhouse District to host a free session for members and post flyers. Offer members a small founding perk.
- **First action:** Visit 2–3 coworking venues, talk to the community manager, propose a member event.
- **Why it fits:** Dense concentration of Personas B and D (builders, freelancers, small teams) in one trusted local space.

#### 10. Alumni & referrals (compounds after cohort 1)
- **What to do:** Build referral into the product. Founding Cohort attendees get a referral incentive for cohort 2; collect testimonials and a short video the day of. Make the alumni community a real, ongoing draw.
- **First action:** Draft the testimonial-capture and referral-offer plan now, so it's ready on day 2 of the cohort.
- **Why it fits:** Word of mouth is the dominant purchase driver for high-trust local purchases. This is the long-term flywheel.

#### 11. Local press & PR
- **What to do:** Pitch a local-angle story — "Pasadena company launches hands-on AI school for non-techies" — to Pasadena Now, Pasadena Star-News, Pasadena Weekly, and neighborhood newsletters. Offer the founder as a local AI expert source.
- **First action:** Write a one-paragraph pitch + founder bio; send to Pasadena Now first.
- **Why it fits:** Local, place-based credibility for a place-based product; reaches Personas A and C who still read local outlets.

## 5. The funnel

```
AWARENESS  →  INTEREST  →  CALL  →  CLOSE  →  ATTEND  →  ADVOCATE
 (talks,      (site:        (Zoom)   (Stripe)  (2 days)   (testimonial
  free          form or               payment              + referral)
  sessions,     direct
  LinkedIn)     Stripe)
```

| Stage | What happens | Tooling | Conversion thinking |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** | Someone hears the founder speak, sees a LinkedIn post, gets a referral, or reads local press. | In-person talks, free sessions, organic LinkedIn, PR. | Goal: get them to the site or a free session. Every talk ends with one clear next step. |
| **Interest** | They visit the site. **Two doors:** ready buyers click **"Reserve your seat — $995"** → Stripe. Everyone else clicks **"Apply / ask a question"** → interest form. | Site, Stripe, interest form. | Don't force the unsure to pay cold. The form lowers friction for the 35–60 audience who want to talk to a human first. Capture email always. |
| **Call** | Form submitters get an email within 24h booking a short Zoom (or phone) call with the founder. | Email + calendar link + Zoom. | **This is where most Founding Cohort seats close.** The call handles objections live, confirms track fit (Zero Experience vs. I Have an Idea), and builds trust. Keep it consultative, not pushy. |
| **Close** | After/at the end of the call, send a **personal payment link**. | Stripe payment link. | Strike while warm — send the link during or immediately after the call. Use scarcity honestly: "24 seats, Founding Cohort." |
| **Attend** | They show up for two days. The product over-delivers. | The cohort itself. | Experience quality *is* the marketing for cohorts 2+. Capture their "before/after" moment. |
| **Advocate** | On day 2, capture a testimonial + video and make the referral ask. | Testimonial form, referral offer, alumni community. | The cheapest cohort-2 seat is a referral from a delighted founding member. Bake the ask into the event. |

**Role of interest form vs. Stripe:**
- **Stripe (direct):** for hot leads — referrals, free-session attendees ready to commit, people who already know Whistle Labs. Fastest path, zero friction.
- **Interest form:** for warm/unsure leads — the majority of a cold-ish 35–60 audience. It buys a conversation, which is where this audience converts. Treat the form as "start a relationship," not "lost sale."

## 6. Free intro workshop / lunch-and-learn playbook

The free session is the heart of the no-ads strategy: it markets and proves the product simultaneously.

### Format
- **Length:** 60–90 minutes. **Title:** "AI in 60 Minutes — No Experience Required" (or hosted-partner co-brand).
- **Structure:**
  1. **(5 min) Honest framing.** "Here's what's real, what's hype, and why you're right on time." Establishes the honest-and-practical voice immediately.
  2. **(35–45 min) One real, hands-on win.** Walk the room through doing one genuinely useful thing live with Claude/ChatGPT — e.g., draft a tough email, build a simple custom workflow, or sketch a tiny app idea. The goal is the *"oh — it clicked"* moment in the room.
  3. **(10 min) The bridge.** "If today clicked, the cohort is the full version — two days, build something real, two tracks." Show the two doors. Name the price plainly: $995, all-inclusive, 24 seats.
  4. **(5–10 min) Soft close + Q&A.** QR code to the interest form and the Stripe link. Offer a brief founder chat to anyone curious.

### How it fills the paid cohort
- **Target conversion:** Aim for ~15–25% of a warm free-session room to enter the funnel (form or buy), and a meaningful share of those to close on the follow-up call.
- **Always capture contact info** (sign-in / QR) — even non-converters become a nurtured list and future referrers.
- **Follow up within 24 hours** with a personal email: thank them, recap the one win, invite to a call, include both links.
- **Run them on a cadence:** one free session every 1–2 weeks during the launch window, rotating venues (chamber, library, coworking, association, sponsor office) to reach all four personas.
- **Math check:** ~6–8 well-attended free sessions (20–30 people each) feeding the funnel should comfortably produce 24 paid seats.

## 7. 30 / 60 / 90-day launch plan

**Today → full Founding Cohort.** Assumes a Fall 2026 cohort with the 90-day window opening roughly early-to-mid summer.

### Days 0–30 — Foundation & first rooms
- **Week 1:**
  - Finalize site funnel (interest form, Stripe links, both CTAs live and tested).
  - Lock the cohort dates and the venue for the 2-day event (see Section 8 for venue thinking).
  - Build the standard talk deck + the free-session deck.
  - Join the **Pasadena Chamber of Commerce**; email membership + events contact.
- **Week 2:**
  - Book the first 3–4 **Rotary/Kiwanis** speaking slots and 2–3 **BNI** guest visits.
  - Email **PCC Community Education** and **Central Library** to propose free sessions.
  - Founder begins **LinkedIn** cadence (3–4 posts/week) + Founding Cohort announcement.
- **Week 3:**
  - Run the **first free lunch & learn** (chamber or library).
  - Pitch **local press** (Pasadena Now first).
  - Set up testimonial + referral capture assets.
- **Week 4:**
  - Review funnel data from session #1; refine deck and follow-up email.
  - Goal checkpoint: **3–5 seats committed/paid**, healthy interest-form list building.

### Days 31–60 — Scale the warm rooms
- Run a **free session every 1–2 weeks**, rotating venues to hit all four personas.
- Deliver the booked **service-club and BNI talks**; book the next wave.
- Land **1–2 association/coworking** partner sessions.
- Founder runs **Zoom close calls** continuously as the form fills; send personal payment links same-day.
- Keep LinkedIn compounding; reshare any press, post free-session moments.
- Goal checkpoint (day 60): **~12–15 seats** committed/paid (cohort half-full or better).

### Days 61–90 — Close the cohort
- Final push of free sessions + targeted personal outreach to warm interest-form leads who haven't closed.
- Use honest scarcity: "Final seats in the Founding Cohort."
- Activate any early referrals; ask network and Whistle Labs contacts directly.
- Confirm all event logistics (catering, room, materials, software setup) for the locked seat count.
- Goal checkpoint (day 90): **24 seats sold** (or waitlist started for cohort 2).
- **Post-close:** keep the funnel warm — overflow interest becomes the cohort-2 waitlist.

## 8. Unit economics & pricing math

**Stated assumptions (LA-market estimates; adjust to actuals):**
- Price: **$995/seat**, all-inclusive. Seats: **24**.
- Format: 2 full days, in person, Pasadena.
- Founder/instructor time is treated as opportunity cost, not cash out (noted separately).

### Gross revenue (full cohort)
- 24 × $995 = **$23,880**

### Estimated variable & event costs

| Cost item | Assumption | Estimate |
|---|---|---|
| Venue / presentation room (2 days) | Mid-range Pasadena meeting/training room or coworking event space, ~$1,000–$2,000/day | **$3,000** |
| Catering — lunches + coffee/snacks | 24 people × 2 days, ~$35/person/day (incl. breakfast/coffee) | **$1,700** |
| Materials & printing | Workbooks, name tags, swag, signage | **$700** |
| Software / tooling | Claude, ChatGPT, Cursor seats/licenses for setup + AV/internet | **$1,200** |
| Payment processing (Stripe ~2.9% + $0.30) | ~2.9% of $23,880 + 24 × $0.30 | **$700** |
| Helper / assistant staff (2 days) | 1 floating helper for hands-on support, 2 days | **$800** |
| Marketing (no-ads) | Chamber membership, printing flyers, misc. — mostly time | **$600** |
| Contingency / misc. | ~10% buffer | **$900** |
| **Total estimated cash costs** | | **≈ $9,600** |

### Margin (full cohort)
- Revenue $23,880 − costs ≈ $9,600 = **≈ $14,280 gross profit**
- **Gross margin ≈ 60%** (cash basis, founder time excluded).
- Founder/instructor time (2 prep-heavy weeks + 2 event days) is the main "soft" cost; even valued generously, the cohort is comfortably profitable.

### Breakeven
- **Fixed-ish event costs** (venue, baseline materials, software, staff, marketing) ≈ **$7,000–$7,500** regardless of headcount.
- **Variable per seat** (catering ~$70 + processing ~$30) ≈ **$100/seat**.
- Breakeven: ~$7,300 fixed ÷ ($995 − $100 per-seat margin) ≈ **~8 seats**.
- **Implication:** the cohort is cash-flow positive at roughly **8 of 24 seats (~33% full).** Everything above 8 seats is mostly profit. This de-risks the Founding Cohort significantly and justifies the relationship-heavy, slower no-ads approach — we don't need a sellout to win.

### Pricing notes
- $995 is positioned as a premium-but-accessible "professional development" price — high enough to signal quality and filter for committed attendees, low enough to be a personal/expensable decision.
- For the Founding Cohort, hold the price; consider a small honest founding perk (lifetime alumni access, cohort-2 referral credit) rather than discounting.

## 10. Risks & mitigations

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Free sessions draw a crowd but few convert.** | Medium | Tighten the in-room "click" moment and the soft close; always capture contacts and follow up by call within 24h; treat free-session attendees as a nurtured list, not one-shot. |
| **Founder time is the bottleneck** (speaking + calls + building product). | High | Standardize the deck and follow-up email; batch close calls; bring in a helper; prioritize the 4 highest-yield channels over doing everything. |
| **Slow start — cohort under-fills near the date.** | Medium | Breakeven is only ~8 seats, so a smaller first cohort still works and still produces testimonials. Pre-decide a minimum viable cohort (e.g., 12) and a fallback date. Start the cohort-2 waitlist with overflow. |
| **Skepticism / "AI hype fatigue" in the audience.** | Medium | Lean into the honest-and-practical pillar; "what's NOT worth your time" builds trust; Whistle Labs credibility anchor; let the free session do the convincing. |
| **Two-day time commitment is a hard no for some.** | Medium | Frame as "save months of false starts"; keep the cohort small so the value is obvious; consider future shorter formats only after cohort 1 proves the model. |
| **Venue / logistics hiccups for a hands-on event.** | Low–Med | Lock venue and AV/internet early; test the Cursor + Claude setup flow before day 1; have a helper and a backup connectivity plan. |
| **Over-reliance on a single channel** (e.g., one chamber). | Medium | Diversify across the Tier 1 four; track conversion by source so spend/time follows what works. |
| **Reputational risk if quality disappoints (small market, word of mouth cuts both ways).** | Low | Over-deliver on cohort 1; small cohort enables high-touch; collect feedback live and fix fast. The same word-of-mouth that's a risk is the primary growth engine if we delight. |

## 11. Appendix — Optional paid acquisition *(secondary; not the strategy)*

> **This is a backup lever, not the plan.** The Founding Cohort should fill through local, relationship-driven outreach. Only consider paid spend if (a) free-session and referral channels under-deliver near the close date, or (b) we want to accelerate cohort 2. Keep budgets small and tightly geo-targeted to Pasadena / Greater LA.

If used, in rough priority:
- **Geo-targeted Meta (Facebook/Instagram) ads** — best fit for the 35–60 audience; target Pasadena + San Gabriel Valley, interests in business/entrepreneurship. Promote the **free session** (a soft offer), not a cold $995 ask.
- **LinkedIn ads** — for Persona B (corporate professionals); expensive, so use only for retargeting site visitors or promoting the free session to a tight job-title/geo audience.
- **Google Search (local intent)** — small budget on high-intent terms (e.g., "AI class Pasadena," "learn AI in person Los Angeles") to capture people already looking.
- **Boosting organic LinkedIn posts** that already performed well, to extend reach cheaply.

**Guardrails:** always drive paid traffic to the *free session* or interest form (not cold checkout); cap spend; measure cost-per-funnel-entry vs. the (near-zero) cost of local channels before scaling anything.
